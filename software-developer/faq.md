## How do I use the engineering ladder?

The engineering ladder is a guide to help you understand what's expected at each level and what growth looks like. Use it as a conversation starter with your manager, not as a rigid checklist.

## Do I need to meet every bullet point to be promoted?

No. The bullets are examples of behaviours, not an exhaustive checklist. Instead of "meeting every point," a promotion is based on consistently demonstrating the Core Focus of the next level across all six competency areas.

## How are the competency areas weighted?

All six areas (Technology, Solution, People, Process, Influence, and Client Satisfaction / Business Value) are equally important. Strength in one area doesn't compensate for a gap in another.

## Can I skip a level?

Generally, no. Each level builds on the previous one. However, new hires may be placed at the level that best matches their demonstrated abilities.

## How often are levels reviewed?

The ladder is officially reviewed and updated annually to ensure our leveling remains calibrated with the broader tech industry and our specific business needs. However, we view this as a "living document".

As the industry evolves—whether through major technical shifts like AI or changing client expectations—we will adapt these competencies to reflect the reality of modern software engineering

## Who decides when I'm ready for the next level?

Promotion decisions are made collaboratively between you, your manager, and leadership. The ladder gives everyone a shared vocabulary for that conversation.

## Is it "up or out"? Do I have to keep getting promoted to stay at the company?

No. We recognize SSD1 and SSD2 as "Terminal Levels." If you are a high-performing, autonomous engineer who loves your specific stack and wants to keep building great features without taking on organizational strategy, you are a vital member of the team and can stay at these levels indefinitely.

## What is a "Terminal Level"?

A terminal level (sometimes called a "career level") is the point in an engineering ladder where an employee is expected to operate autonomously and is no longer required to strive for further promotion to stay with the company. Being at a terminal level does not mean you stop growing; it means your performance is measured against the high bar of a Senior Engineer.

## How often can I expect a promotion?

Promotions aren't about time served; they are about Scope and Impact. You should be consistently operating at the next level's requirements for at least 6 months before a formal promotion is considered.

## I am a deep specialist in a specific platform. Can I reach the SSD2 level without becoming a cross-stack generalist?

Yes. The SSD2 level is designed for Domain Authorities.  We value deep specialization. To reach SSD2, you must be a go-to expert for your primary tech stack. You don’t need to be a generalist to be a master of your craft.

## Why is "Client Satisfaction" in a technical ladder?

Because code doesn't exist in a vacuum. As you progress, your ability to understand why the work matters and to de-risk client relationships through technical excellence becomes as important as the code itself. High-level engineers act as Strategic Partners who translate technical risks into business strategy.

## How do I get exposure to different parts of the stack if I’m busy with my current tickets?

Growth often happens in the "spaces between." Actively participate in pairing sessions as an observer, contribute to code reviews outside your immediate tasks, and use team ceremonies to listen to how Seniors handle complex integrations. Showing an eagerness to learn beyond your assigned tickets is a key indicator of SD2 and SD3 potential.

## If the ladder suggests I should require "limited feedback", does that mean I should stop asking questions?

Not at all. "Limited feedback" applies to the day-to-day, mundane tasks you’ve we expect you to handle with high autonomy. However, as you stretch into impactful, higher-risk work, seeking feedback is a sign of maturity. We want you to use senior developers as a sounding board for complex logic to ensure we’re building things right the first time.
