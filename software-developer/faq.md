## How do I use the engineering ladder?

The engineering ladder is a guide to help you understand what's expected at each level and what growth looks like. Use it as a conversation starter with your manager, not as a rigid checklist.

## How are decisions about promotions made?

Growth is based on Competency-Driven Progression. This means we evaluate how you operate on a daily basis, not just the volume of tasks you close. To progress "up" the ladder, you should be ready to demonstrate that you have fully embedded the baseline fundamentals of that level into your regular behaviour.

## Do I need to meet every bullet point to be promoted?

No. The bullets are examples of behaviours, not an exhaustive checklist. Instead of "meeting every point," a promotion is based on consistently demonstrating the Core Focus of the next level across all six competency areas.

## How are the competency areas weighted?

All six areas (Technology, Solution, People, Process, Influence, and Client Satisfaction / Business Value) are equally important. Strength in one area doesn't compensate for a gap in another.

## Can I skip a level?

Generally, no. Each level builds on the previous one. However, new hires may be placed at the level that best matches their demonstrated abilities.

## How often are levels reviewed?

The ladder is officially reviewed and updated annually to ensure our leveling remains calibrated with the broader tech industry and our specific business needs. However, we view this as a "living document".

As the industry evolves, whether through major technical shifts like AI or changing client expectations, we will adapt these competencies to reflect the reality of modern software engineering

## Who decides when I'm ready for the next level?

Promotion decisions are made collaboratively between you, your manager, and leadership. The ladder gives everyone a shared vocabulary for that conversation.

## Is it "up or out"? Do I have to keep getting promoted to stay at the company?

No. We recognize SSD1 and SSD2 as "Terminal Levels." If you are a high-performing, autonomous engineer who loves your specific stack and wants to keep building great features without taking on organizational strategy, you are a vital member of the team and can stay at these levels indefinitely.

## What is a "Terminal Level"?

A terminal level (sometimes called a "career level") is the point in an engineering ladder where an employee is expected to operate autonomously and is no longer required to strive for further promotion to stay with the company. Being at a terminal level does not mean you stop growing; it means your performance is measured against the high bar of a Senior Engineer.

## How often can I expect a promotion?

Promotions aren't about time served; they are about Scope and Impact. You should be consistently operating at the next level's requirements for at least 6 months before a formal promotion is considered.

That said, we define guideline ranges, not deadlines, for typical progression:

* **SD1 to SD2**: 12–18 months (learning the ropes, adopting habits)
* **SD2 to SD3**: 18–24 months (building independence, solidifying component ownership)
* **SD3 to SSD1**: 24+ months (shifting from expert to leader and lifecycle master)

These ranges account for the fact that some people are high-growth and others are steady-state. Both are normal. If you feel like you've been at a level longer than expected, talk to your ESM / Manager, that conversation is exactly what the framework is designed to support.

Note, these timelines are "years in role" moreso than overall "years of experience".

## What if I've been at the same level for a long time?

First: don't panic. Some engineers choose to stay at a level, particularly SD3 or SSD1, because they love the work and are excellent at it. That's a valid, respected path (see "Terminal Levels" above).

However, if you feel stuck rather than settled, raise it with your ESM / Manager. The two most common reasons for a longer-than-expected stay at a level are:

1. **A performance gap**: There may be specific competency areas where you haven't yet demonstrated the next level's expectations. Your ESM can help you build a targeted plan.
2. **An opportunity gap**: The projects you've been on may not have provided the right context to demonstrate next-level work. This is a two-way street. Seek out stretch opportunities and make your ambitions known, and your ESM will work to align you with projects that give you room to grow.

Your ESM / Manager's job is to figure out which of these applies and work with you on a path forward. If you've been at a level for a while and no one has had this conversation with you, ask for it.

## I am a deep specialist in a specific platform. Can I reach the SSD2 level without becoming a cross-stack generalist?

Yes. The SSD2 level is designed for Domain Authorities.  We value deep specialization. To reach SSD2, you must be a go-to expert for your primary tech stack. You don’t need to be a generalist to be a master of your craft.

## Why is "Client Satisfaction" in a technical ladder?

Because code doesn't exist in a vacuum. As you progress, your ability to understand why the work matters and to de-risk client relationships through technical excellence becomes as important as the code itself. High-level engineers act as Strategic Partners who translate technical risks into business strategy.

## How do I get exposure to different parts of the stack if I’m busy with my current tickets?

Growth often happens in the "spaces between." Actively participate in pairing sessions as an observer, contribute to code reviews outside your immediate tasks, and use team ceremonies to listen to how Seniors handle complex integrations. Showing an eagerness to learn beyond your assigned tickets is a key indicator of SD2 and SD3 potential.

## If the ladder suggests I should require "limited feedback", does that mean I should stop asking questions?

Not at all. "Limited feedback" applies to the day-to-day, mundane tasks you’ve we expect you to handle with high autonomy. However, as you stretch into impactful, higher-risk work, seeking feedback is a sign of maturity. We want you to use senior developers as a sounding board for complex logic to ensure we’re building things right the first time.

## What counts as "one jump"?

A technology where the engineer's existing foundational patterns transfer with a ramp-up, not a from-scratch relearn. Examples of one jump: one component framework to another (React → Svelte/Vue), one cloud provider to another (AWS → GCP), one native mobile platform to another. Examples of a real jump (not adjacent): frontend → data engineering, web → embedded, application dev → ML systems. The test isn't "have they used it" — it's "do the patterns they've mastered carry, such that we'd trust them to lead, not just contribute."

## Does the technology someone works in affect their level?

No — not the technology's market value, anyway. The ladder measures the engineer: their capability, the scope they operate at, and the impact they have. It does not measure how fashionable, scarce, or strategically central their stack is. Two things follow:

We don't lower a level because the stack is legacy, niche, or off-strategy. The engineer rarely chooses the assignment, we do. Docking their level for a staffing decision punishes the people doing the maintenance the business still depends on, and teaches everyone to flee that work the moment they can.

We don't raise a level because the stack is hot or rare. Skill scarcity and strategic value are real — but they're levers for compensation, retention, and staffing, not for level. Conflating them inflates titles and corrodes the ladder's meaning.

**What does matter, in any stack**: whether the engineer demonstrates the behaviors of the level. Deep expertise in a current stack is a current-level signal. The ability to evaluate, predict, and lead beyond it is a next-level signal and that holds whether the stack is on the path to legacy or the newest thing in the building.

## Why did "SSD3" become "Staff Software Developer"?

Simple answer, to stop the level from reading as "the next rung on a ladder". SSD1 to SSD2 is already a real shift, not an automatic progression, and the move to Staff is a larger one still. The name "Staff Software Developer" carries no implied "+1," so it stops framing the level as something you age into and starts framing it as something you cross into. But things are rarely simple...

This is a different kind of role, not a bigger version of the same one. A Staff Software Developer is not "a really senior lead dev." The scope is wider, the impact is different in kind, and a meaningful part of the job is no longer development in the hands-on sense. They set technical standards and direction for the broader staff, translate technical reality into business strategy, sponsor the growth of other senior engineers, and are trusted to lead almost any project that comes through the door. Measuring that against "writes excellent code and leads a project" misses most of what the level actually is. The expectation changes, so the title should say so.

This also aligns us with how the industry frames these people. "Staff" is the widely understood signal for an individual contributor operating past the senior level, with company-wide rather than team or project scope. Using the common name makes the expectation legible both inside REDspace and to anyone who knows the broader market: this person is operating at the next level and has proven they can work there, not waiting in line for a number to tick up.
