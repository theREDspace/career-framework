title: Frequently Asked Questions
---

## How do I use the QA ladder?

The QA career ladder is a framework to help you understand the expectations at each level of the QA track. Use it to identify where you are today and what skills and behaviors you need to develop to reach the next level.

## What are the QA levels?

The QA track has five levels across three tiers: QA Specialist (QA1, QA2), Senior QA Specialist (SQA1, SQA2), and QA Team Lead (QL). Each level builds on the previous one, expanding your scope of impact.

## How is the QA ladder different from the engineering ladder?

The QA ladder focuses on the same competency areas — Technology, Solution, People, Process, Influence, and Client Satisfaction / Business Value. They've simply been tailored to illustrate the career opportunities and role competencies for the QA discipline.

## How do I move up a level?

Growth happens by consistently demonstrating the competencies of the next level. Talk to your lead about where you are and what areas to focus on. The ladder is a guide, not a checklist — sustained demonstration of higher-level behaviors is what matters.

## Who should I talk to about my progression?

Your QA Team Lead is the best person to discuss your career progression with. Use the ladder as a shared reference point during 1:1s and performance reviews.

## Do I need to meet every bullet point to be promoted?

No. The bullets are examples of behaviours, not a strict checklist. You should be consistently demonstrating the *spirit* of a level across all six competency areas before moving up.

## How are the competency areas weighted?

All six areas (Technology, Solution, People, Process, Influence, and Client Satisfaction / Business Value) are equally important. Strength in one area doesn't compensate for a gap in another.

## Can I skip a level?

Generally, no. Each level builds on the previous one. New hires will be placed at the level that best matches their demonstrated abilities.

## How often are levels reviewed?

The ladder is officially reviewed and updated annually to ensure our leveling remains calibrated with the broader tech industry and our specific business needs. However, we view this as a "living document".

As the industry evolves—whether through major shifts in testing practices, rubrics/methodologies, or changing organizational expectations—we will adapt these competencies to reflect the reality of contemporary quality assurance.

## Who decides when I'm ready for the next level?

Promotion decisions are made collaboratively between you, your QA Team Lead, and leadership. The ladder gives everyone a shared vocabulary for that conversation.

## Is it "up or out"? Do I have to keep getting promoted to stay at the company?

No. We recognize SQA1 and SQA2 as "Terminal Levels." If you are a high-performing, autonomous QA specialist who excels at ensuring quality and wants to keep building robust testing practices without taking on organizational leadership, you are a vital member of the team and can stay at these levels indefinitely.

## How often can I expect a promotion?

Promotions aren't about time served; they are about Scope and Impact. You should be consistently operating at the next level's requirements for at least 6 months before a formal promotion is considered.

## Why is "Client Satisfaction" in a QA ladder?

Because quality assurance doesn't exist in a vacuum. As you progress, your ability to understand why the work matters and to de-risk client relationships through proactive quality practices becomes as important as the testing itself. High-level QA specialists act as Strategic Partners who translate quality risks into business strategy.

## How do I get exposure to different testing approaches if I'm busy with my current assignments?

Growth often happens in the "spaces between." Actively participate in pairing sessions as an observer, contribute to test plan reviews outside your immediate tasks, and use team ceremonies to listen to how Senior QA specialists handle complex quality challenges. Showing an eagerness to learn beyond your assigned tasks is a key indicator of QA2 and SQA1 potential.

## If the ladder suggests I should require "limited feedback", does that mean I should stop asking questions?

Not at all. "Limited feedback" applies to the day-to-day, routine tasks we expect you to handle with high autonomy. However, as you stretch into impactful, higher-risk work, seeking feedback is a sign of maturity. We want you to use senior QA specialists as a sounding board for complex testing strategies to ensure we're building quality practices right the first time.
